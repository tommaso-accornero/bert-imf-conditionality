## 3.1 Data Sources

For this analysis, I relied on two main data sources: the IMF Monitor as training data and MONA as the inference dataset for the 2020–2024 extension. The IMF Monitor database (Kentikelenis & Stubbs 2023) is particularly valuable, as it contains 35,401 conditions with no missing values, covering the period from 1980 to 2019 and spanning 134 countries. What sets this dataset apart is that it was hand-coded by independent researchers and, unlike the original Kentikelenis et al. (2016) database, includes 13 categories rather than 7. This greater granularity at the condition level is an advantage for my analysis, though it does introduce some challenges. Some categories are quite ambiguous and therefore more difficult to classify, which could lower BERT's macro-F1 score. For instance, the Institutional category (INS) overlaps linguistically with fiscal transparency conditions (FP), civil service conditions (LAB), and public enterprise governance (SOE), reflecting a semantic ambiguity that is reflected in its F1 score of 0.699, the lowest among all 13 categories. The MONA dataset, on the other hand, is the IMF’s own self-reported record of conditions. While MONA covers 2000 to 2025 and includes 56,710 rows, I focused on the 2020-2024 window, the period immediately following the IMF Monitor's 2019 endpoint. I identified 9,863 conditions after excluding 1,279 future-dated observations from active programs that were not yet completed at the time of data collection. Unlike the IMF Monitor, MONA classifies conditions using 33 Economic Descriptors rather than the 13 IMF Monitor categories, a difference addressed through the mapping procedure described in Section 3.3. I chose to use Test Date as the assessment year rather than the approval year because the approval year collapses all conditions from a multi-year arrangement into a single program-year, artificially inflating per-year condition counts and obscuring the actual assessment timeline.

It is important to recognize the fundamental difference between the IMF Monitor, an independent academic reconstruction based on LOI/MEFP primary documents, and MONA, an institutional self-report. MONA is subject to self-reporting bias and uses different counting conventions (54.5 versus 16.3 conditions per program-year), which could introduce bias into the analysis. However, I consider this manageable, since raw condition counts are not directly comparable across sources. Instead, I rely on scope and composition measures, the number of distinct policy areas covered and the distribution of conditions across categories, when making cross-source comparisons.

I chose to use MONA because no automated pipeline currently exists for extracting structured condition data from LOI/MEFP documents at scale. Stubbs (pers. comm., 2026) has identified it as a priority for future research. In addition, MONA is the standard structured source for post-2019 IMF conditions and has been empirically validated here via seam analysis and lookup agreement.


## 3.2 Bert Classification Procedure

Condition texts underwent minimal preprocessing: leading and trailing whitespace was removed, internal whitespace was collapsed, and line breaks were eliminated. All texts were converted to lowercase. Stopword removal and stemming were not applied because BERT's pre-training encodes word-level statistical regularities, and aggressive cleaning would eliminate syntactic cues that contribute to classification accuracy.

The training corpus underwent two deduplication passes. First, 13 fully identical rows (matching across all columns) were removed during data preparation. Second, 1,076 conditions with identical text across different arrangements but within the same country-year were removed before the train/validation/test split. This step is critical because retaining these conditions would allow BERT to memorize text-to-label mappings rather than learn generalizable patterns. A pre-removal check found 13 condition texts (0.07%) with inconsistent labels across programs; the first occurrence was retained in each case. The resulting 34,325 training conditions were split 70/15/15 into training (24,027), validation (5,149), and test (5,149) sets, stratified by 13-category labels. A limitation of this design is within-arrangement clustering: conditions from the same program may appear in both training and test sets, which may modestly inflate reported performance.

The bert-base-uncased model (Devlin et al. 2019) was selected over domain-specific alternatives such as FinBERT because IMF conditions span economics, governance, social policy, and institutional reform, making general pre-training preferable to vocabulary optimized for narrower domains. Larger models such as BERT-large and RoBERTa-large were not pursued due to computational constraints. An encoder-only architecture was preferred over generative models because supervised sequence classification does not require the additional complexity of decoder-based approaches. The classifier fine-tunes bert-base-uncased for sequence classification with MAX_LENGTH set to 128, BATCH_SIZE to 32, EPOCHS to 4, and a learning rate of 3e-5. The training data exhibits severe class imbalance (FIN = 8,516 examples; ENV = 199, a 43:1 ratio), so balanced class weights were applied throughout training. The ENV class received a weight of 13.3, while the FIN class received a weight of 0.32. Without this correction, the model would systematically underperform on minority categories, which are particularly relevant for detecting mission creep.

Two baseline models, TF-IDF with Logistic Regression and TF-IDF with Random Forest, were trained on the same splits to establish a performance floor (macro-F1 = 0.774 and 0.733, respectively). A parallel model was fine-tuned on the collapsed 7-category scheme; classification performance for both models is reported in Section 4.1.

Before applying the classifier to MONA 2020–2024, two validation exercises were conducted to address concerns that a model trained on 1980–2019 hand-coded data may not generalize across source and time boundaries. First, BERT was applied to 207 hand-coded IMF Monitor program-years from 2015 to 2019. Program-level scope scores computed from BERT predictions matched hand-coded scores exactly in 91.3% of cases and were within one policy area in 99.0% of cases, with a mean absolute error of 0.097 and a bias of +0.077. This positive bias indicates that BERT marginally overstates scope relative to human coders, which is a conservative direction for the mission creep test. Second, the 100 MONA 2020–2024 conditions were manually coded by the author. BERT agreed with the author's primary judgment in 86% of cases (89% when adjusted for genuinely ambiguous conditions). Notably, there were zero disagreements at the GOVERNANCE/FISCAL boundary, which is the most consequential for the mission creep analysis.

## 3.3 Category Design Decisions

Conditions are classified according to the action of the policy instrument rather than the stated objective. For instance, if a condition requires the government to set a ceiling on net domestic assets, it is assigned to DEB, regardless of whether the program frames it as a fiscal consolidation measure or as a precondition for poverty reduction. This instrument-focused approach ensures that the classification scheme remains reproducible and is insulated from the ambiguity frequently present in official program rhetoric. Such methodological rigor is particularly important when assessing whether program rhetoric aligns with actual policy content.

The 13-category scheme from Kentikelenis and Stubbs (2023) is replicated to maintain comparability with the existing literature and to preserve full granularity for evaluating BERT's performance. For the mission creep regression, these categories are consolidated into seven groups to reduce data sparsity and focus the analysis: FISCAL (FP, RTP, DEB), FINANCIAL (FIN), EXTERNAL (EXT), PRIVATISATION (SOE, PRI), LABOUR (LAB), SOCIAL (SP, POV), and GOVERNANCE (INS, ENV, OTH), following guidance from Stubbs (pers. comm., 2026).

The inclusion of the GOVERNANCE category warrants justification. Institutional capacity, environmental policy, and other structural measures are most distant from the IMF's original Bretton Woods mandate of macroeconomic stabilisation and are not considered core fiscal or financial conditions. The increasing presence of these measures in IMF programmes over time provides empirical evidence for mission creep. Therefore, these measures are grouped under GOVERNANCE to ensure that the signal of mission creep remains identifiable.

The same rationale applies to assigning DEB to FISCAL. Debt management conditions, including borrowing ceilings, debt reporting obligations, and debt sustainability frameworks, are central fiscal compliance instruments. Placing DEB in GOVERNANCE would conflate mandatory debt compliance with discretionary institutional reform, thereby undermining the interpretability of GOVERNANCE as an indicator of mission creep. Following this assignment, FISCAL accounts for 47.2% of the training data, which remains within the acceptable range for BERT training.

To translate MONA conditions into the 13-category scheme, a manual mapping of descriptors was conducted. MONA employs 33 proprietary Economic Descriptors: 25 were mapped with high confidence and 8 with medium confidence, where the primary assignment was defensible but an alternative could equally be justified. In medium-confidence cases, the instrument principle was applied. For example, civil service reforms were assigned to LAB rather than INS, as the IMF's operational focus is on wage bills and staffing levels, which constitute labor market interventions rather than governance reforms. These decisions were reviewed in consultation with a supervisor.

For MONA 2020–2024, two independent classification outputs were generated: lookup labels from the descriptor mapping and BERT predictions based on the condition text. The lookup labels serve as a validation benchmark, while BERT predictions are used as the primary classification for the mission creep analysis. The two methods agree in 76.9% of cases at the 7-category level. Notably, BERT assigns 13.2% GOVERNANCE to MONA 2020–2024, while the lookup assigns 14.4%. Both approaches, one based on text and the other on taxonomy, indicate that GOVERNANCE has approximately tripled from its 3.9% share in the training data. Manual validation of 100 MONA conditions subsequently confirmed zero disagreements at the GOVERNANCE/FISCAL boundary, the boundary most consequential for the mission creep finding.

## 3.4 Mission Creep Operationalization

The dependent variable is defined as the number of distinct policy areas addressed by each IMF programme-year, measured by counting the unique categories present among all conditions attached to an arrangement within a given year. This metric, ranging from 1 to 13 under the 13-category classification, reflects the breadth of engagement rather than the volume of conditions. For example, a programme containing 40 fiscal conditions but no structural conditions is assigned a scope of 1, whereas a programme with 8 conditions spanning 8 categories is assigned a scope of 8. This distinction is critical, as the analysis evaluates whether the IMF's reach expanded across policy domains, rather than whether it increased its demands within a single domain.

The outcome variable is a count and was initially modeled using negative binomial regression to account for potential overdispersion. However, the data did not support this approach: the residual dispersion ratio for the full specification was 0.227, indicating underdispersion relative to the Poisson assumption. Consequently, the negative binomial model was empirically rejected, and a Poisson Generalized Linear Model (GLM) was employed as Poisson Pseudo-Maximum Likelihood (PPML), following Wooldridge (2010). Under PPML, consistency requires only a correctly specified conditional mean, the expected scope given covariates equals exp(Xβ), rather than a fully correct distributional assumption. The presence of underdispersion is advantageous in this context, as Poisson standard errors are conservative when the outcome is underdispersed.

Additionally, all standard errors are estimated using cluster-robust variance at the country level to account for within-country correlation across programme-years. Coefficients are reported as Incidence Rate Ratios (IRR = exp(β)), where an IRR of 1.010 on year_c indicates a 1.0% increase in the expected number of distinct policy areas per additional year.

The primary specification, M3_extended, regresses n_policy_areas on year_c (year centered at 1990), post_2002 and post_2009 (binary step indicators for the two reform waves), arrangement_type, log_n_conditions, log_loan_size, region, transition_economy, repeat_borrower, log_gdp_pc, and any_crisis. Each control variable addresses a specific potential confounder.

Log_n_conditions is particularly important, as a greater number of conditions mechanically increases the likelihood of spanning additional categories. Therefore, the year_c coefficient should be interpreted as capturing scope expansion conditional on program volume, rather than reflecting the raw upward trend in the number of conditions. Arrangement_type accounts for the broader scope of poverty-focused programs (ECF, PRGF, ESAF) compared to standard Stand-By Arrangements. Log loan size controls for the possibility that larger programs require broader conditionality across more policy areas; this variable is available only for IMF Monitor rows and is absent from MONA observations. The region variable controls for differences in IMF departmental engagement patterns. The transition_economy dummy identifies 241 program-years in the Former Soviet Union and Eastern Europe between 1991 and 2005 that were unusually broad by historical standards. Repeat borrowers, defined as countries with 15 or more years under IMF programs, are flagged separately, as these long-term borrowers may exhibit entrenched structural conditions that are independent of temporal trends. Log GDP per capita and the crisis dummy (Laeven & Valencia 2018) are included to test whether scope expansion is attributable to changes in borrower composition or crisis-driven lending, rather than institutional mission creep.

The sample comprises 2,086 program-years from the IMF Monitor dataset (1980–2019). Data from MONA 2020–2024 are excluded from the primary regression, as BERT classification errors are non-random and could introduce a spurious post-2020 trend. The complete 1980–2024 sample is analyzed as a robustness check. Variance inflation factor (VIF) analysis for M3_extended indicated no severe multicollinearity; the highest value was 7.03 for year_c, attributable to its correlation with the post-2002 and post-2009 step indicators rather than to substantive multicollinearity. All VIF values remained below the threshold of 10 (see Appendix A for the full table). Structural break tests are used in addition to level-shift dummies. Instead of testing for intercept shifts at 2002 and 2009, these tests assess whether the entire coefficient vector changed, providing a more rigorous evaluation of whether the reforms altered the relationship between program characteristics and scope.
